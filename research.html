PhD Dissertation Research

Probing the Signal Transduction Mechanism of Blue Light-Activated Adenylate Cyclase OaPAC

Research Overview

My doctoral research focused on understanding how blue light activation of the BLUF photoreceptor domain in OaPAC triggers conformational changes that ultimately lead to adenylate cyclase activation and cAMP production.

Using unnatural amino acid mutagenesis, I replaced key amino acids with fluorinated analogs to modulate their chemical properties and probe their roles in the photoactivation mechanism.

Key Findings:
  • Demonstrated that Y6 acidity is critical for photocycle completion
  • Identified concerted proton-coupled electron transfer (PCET) mechanism
  • Mapped signal transduction pathway from BLUF to AC domain
  • Characterized structural dynamics using time-resolved spectroscopy

Figure 1: Crystal structure of OaPAC showing the BLUF domain (blue) and adenylate cyclase domain (orange) with bound FAD and ATP.

Experimental Approaches

Protein Engineering

Site-directed mutagenesis and unnatural amino acid incorporation using orthogonal aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases to introduce fluorotyrosine analogs at position Y6.

Time-Resolved Spectroscopy

Ultrafast time-resolved infrared (TRIR) and multiple probe spectroscopy (TRMPS) to monitor photochemical intermediates and structural dynamics.

Enzyme Kinetics

Development of coupled enzyme assays to quantify adenylate cyclase activity and determine kinetic parameters under light and dark conditions.

Figure 2: FTIR difference spectra showing structural changes in OaPAC domains upon light activation.

Key Results

My research revealed that the photoactivation mechanism of OaPAC involves a concerted proton-coupled electron transfer (PCET) from the conserved Y6 to the excited FAD chromophore.

Critical Discovery

Fluorination of Y6 that increased phenol acidity beyond a threshold halted the photocycle at the FAD•⁻ intermediate, preventing light-state formation and enzyme activation.

Mechanistic Insight

Only variants with Y6 pKa values ≥7.8 retained photoinduced catalytic activity, demonstrating the importance of proton availability for successful signal transduction.
